*AI is flattening companies. Inside AI, researchers found the opposite.*

For the past several years, "flattening" has become one of corporate America's favorite words.

Amazon has described recent restructuring as an effort to **reduce layers, increase ownership, and remove bureaucracy**. CEO Andy Jassy has repeatedly targeted what he considers excessive management and unnecessary process. In January 2026, Amazon announced another **16,000 job cuts** after an earlier round affecting **14,000 positions**.

Meta has taken the idea further. In May, the company told employees that it would **eliminate managerial roles** while transferring **7,000 workers** into initiatives related to **AI workflows**. Layoffs, transfers, closed positions, and organizational changes together affected a substantial part of the company.

The logic appears straightforward.

Middle managers collect updates, summarize information, schedule meetings, monitor progress, relay instructions, and coordinate teams. **AI systems can already perform many of these tasks faster and at lower cost.** Remove the messengers, connect employees more directly to senior leadership, and let software handle the administrative traffic.

**Fewer layers should mean less distortion, lower cost, and faster decisions.**

Then Anthropic published **a finding that complicates this story**.

Researchers examining several Claude models identified a small internal representational structure that seemed to perform precisely the kind of function companies are trying to remove: it **selected a limited amount of information**, **made that information broadly available**, and allowed separate downstream processes to use **the same intermediate result**.

Anthropic called it the **J-space**.

The finding does not prove that companies need traditional middle managers. A language model is not a corporation, and a pattern in a neural network is not an employee.

But it raises a harder question:

*Is the middle layer merely an obstacle to information flow, or is some form of middle layer necessary for a complex system to behave as a whole?*

## Why Everything Cannot Talk to Everything

A large language model contains enormous amounts of distributed numerical activity. Different parts of the network process syntax, associations, factual patterns, context, intermediate calculations, and possible continuations.

The naive assumption is that better intelligence should come from making all this information available everywhere.

**But unlimited access is not coordination.**

If every partial signal had equal influence over every later operation, the result would not be perfect transparency. **It would be congestion.** Important evidence, irrelevant associations, incomplete calculations, and noise would all compete for the same downstream attention.

**Complex systems need selection.**

Anthropic's researchers developed a method called the Jacobian lens, or J-lens, to identify certain internal representations that could later influence verbal output. Sparse combinations of these representations formed what the researchers defined as the **J-space**.

The **J-space** was not a physical compartment or a separately designed module. It was an experimentally identified subset of the model's representational activity. The method is approximate, and the researchers explicitly state that it captures the underlying structure incompletely.

What made the **J-space** important was not its size. It accounted for **less than ten percent of activation variance** and typically contained representations corresponding to **only a few dozen concepts**.

What made it important was its position.

Many more components of the network could write information into these representations and read information from them than from ordinary representations. In some parts of the network, the difference was **approximately a hundredfold**.

The **J-space** acted less like a warehouse and more like a **common format**.

## A Shared Representation Can Coordinate Different Tasks

The researchers tested whether **J-space** activity merely reflected decisions made elsewhere or whether later computation actually used it.

In one experiment, they replaced a representation associated with France with one associated with China. After the intervention, separate downstream tasks produced Beijing as the capital, Chinese as the language, Asia as the continent, and yuan as the currency.

**Four different operations used the same altered representation.**

This distinction matters.

A system could store four separate facts:

- France → Paris
- France → French
- France → Europe
- France → euro

Or it could establish a shared intermediate representation of the country and allow several processes to operate on it.

**The second arrangement has a middle layer.**

It creates **one state that can be reused across different tasks**. Instead of every subsystem independently reconstructing the entire context, **selected information becomes available in a form that several subsystems can understand**.

**The strongest evidence came from suppression experiments.**

When researchers repeatedly removed the most active **J-space** contents, the models retained much of their routine performance. They could still generate fluent language, classify sentiment, extract facts from passages, and answer many multiple-choice questions.

But **performance on multi-step reasoning fell close to zero**. Summarization and rhyming poetry also **deteriorated sharply**.

**Local capabilities remained. Flexible coordination did not.**

This is the important finding. A complex system can continue producing competent local output after losing much of the structure that combines those capabilities into **coherent, reusable judgment**.

## The Case Against Middle Management Is Only Half Right

Companies are not wrong to attack bureaucracy.

A middle layer can delay decisions, soften bad news, multiply approvals, protect its own status, and translate clear information into empty management language. Some managers do little more than transfer information between people who could communicate directly.

**That kind of middle layer is friction.**

But companies often confuse **a poor implementation with an unnecessary function**.

The real function of a useful middle layer is **not to move messages upward and downward**. It is to **change the form of information**.

Raw information arrives from different places:

- customer complaints;
- engineering constraints;
- missed targets;
- operational failures;
- staff conflicts;
- regulatory concerns;
- competitor activity.

These signals are incomplete, uneven, and expressed in incompatible professional languages. Senior executives cannot inspect all of them directly. Nor can every team continually follow the work of every other team.

Someone—or something—must decide:

- which local problem has company-wide significance;
- which detail can be discarded;
- which uncertainty must remain visible;
- which teams need the information;
- and which conflict requires a decision at a higher level.

**This is not transmission. It is selection, compression, translation, and routing.**

The **J-space** research does not show that human managers are the best way to perform these functions. It shows something more limited but more fundamental:

*Removing an intermediate structure does not remove the need for information to become shared, compressed, and reusable.*

When companies cut management layers, **that work does not automatically vanish**. It may move into executive calendars, cross-functional meetings, dashboards, internal chat, project-management systems, or unrecognized coordination performed by individual employees.

**An organization chart can become flatter while the underlying coordination burden becomes larger.**

## Vibe Coding Reveals Where the Middle Layer Goes

Software development provides a visible example of this migration.

The popular description of **vibe coding** is simple: a user describes an application in natural language, and **an AI system writes the code**.

**That description hides most of the work.**

"Build an employee management system" **is not a software specification**. It does not establish user roles, permissions, data relationships, deletion rules, error states, authentication boundaries, or acceptance criteria.

A frontend can look complete while having no reliable backend. A database can store information while enforcing the wrong relationships. A login screen can function while exposing records to the wrong users.

**Generating code is a local capability. Producing a coherent application is a coordination problem.**

This is where [AutoCoder.cc](https://www.autocoder.cc/platform?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=reports&utm_campaign=MiddleLayer "Build full-stack apps end to end with AutoCoder.cc") becomes relevant—**not because it eliminates the middle layer**, but because its documented workflow **makes that layer visible**.

AutoCoder.cc presents itself as an end-to-end platform that generates frontend interfaces, backend logic, data persistence, authentication, deployment, and exportable source code from natural-language requirements. More revealingly, its build process is divided into requirement input, a requirement list, demo generation, and iterative demo updates. It also expands brief prompts into more structured descriptions before generation.

**That sequence is the product.**

The valuable step is not merely converting English into JavaScript. It is **converting an ambiguous intention into a shared product state** that several technical components can use.

A request must be transformed into:

- pages and user flows;
- data entities and relationships;
- permissions and business rules;
- interface states;
- backend operations;
- deployment assumptions.

Traditional software teams distribute this translation across product managers, designers, architects, developers, testers, and operations staff. A platform such as AutoCoder.cc attempts to encode part of that translation into software.

**The middle layer has not been removed. It has been productized.**

That does not eliminate the underlying difficulty.

Research on **vibe coding** shows **a persistent gap between generating software and verifying it**. In a 2026 survey of 162 users, non-coders, novices, and professional developers broadly recognized the risks of AI-generated code, but their ability to test and verify outputs differed substantially. Another study of more than 22,000 pull requests found that less-experienced vibe coders produced larger changes but received **4.52 times more review comments**, had **31 percent lower acceptance rates**, and waited substantially longer for resolution.

This is what real use feels like when the invisible middle layer is weak.

The first version arrives quickly. It may even look finished. Then a new permission rule breaks an earlier workflow. A database change affects several screens. Fixing one component introduces a regression somewhere else. The user can describe the desired surface result but may not know which shared constraint has been violated underneath.

**Speed at the local level increases the value of coordination at the system level.**

The serious question for AutoCoder.cc and every similar platform is therefore **not how many roles it can claim to replace**. It is **how well it preserves a coherent product state while requirements change**.

Can the system remember which decisions are fixed? Can it detect contradictions between a new request and the existing data model? Can it distinguish a cosmetic modification from an architectural change? Can it verify that frontend behavior, backend rules, and database permissions still agree?

**Those capabilities—not code volume—determine whether vibe coding remains rapid prototyping or becomes reliable software development.**

## The Middle Layer Is Becoming Infrastructure

The corporate campaign against middle management is based on a real diagnosis. Too much organizational information is repeatedly copied, summarized, approved, and delayed without becoming more useful.

But **the cure is not the elimination of every intermediate layer**.

The useful distinction is between **two kinds of middle structure**.

**One merely passes information along. It creates delay.**

The other **converts scattered local signals into a compact state** that different parts of the system can use. **It creates coordination.**

**The first can often be removed. The second must be preserved somewhere.**

Claude's **J-space** suggests that even a highly distributed computational system may depend on **a narrow, privileged format for flexible coordination**. Corporate restructuring shows that companies are trying to reduce the human roles historically associated with that function. AutoCoder.cc shows one place the function may be moving: **into software that translates an idea into shared requirements, architecture, implementation, and deployment**.

The future organization may contain fewer traditional managers. The future software team may contain fewer traditional specialists.

**But the middle layer will remain.**

It will appear as **protocols, models, workflows, product state, verification systems, and AI infrastructure**.
