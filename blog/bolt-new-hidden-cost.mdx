## The Three Layers of Platform Lock-In

Platform lock-in in AI coding tools operates across three distinct layers, each with its own migration cost.

<Frame caption="The three layers of platform lock-in—infrastructure, code architecture, and organizational knowledge—create compounding migration costs.">
  ![Diagram of the three layers of AI platform lock-in: infrastructure, code architecture, and knowledge and workflow](/images/blog/bolt-new-hidden-cost/02.png)
</Frame>

### Layer 1: Infrastructure Lock-In

When you build on Bolt.new, your application runs in their browser-based environment. Your hosting, deployment pipeline, and runtime environment are all managed by the platform. This convenience comes with a price: you cannot easily export your application to run on your own infrastructure or third-party hosting platforms without significant reconfiguration.

Traditional website builders like Wix and Squarespace have faced criticism for years over this exact issue. The difference? AI coding tools generate actual code, creating the illusion of portability. But as enterprise teams have discovered, ["AI lock-in is worse than cloud lock-in because of API dependency, data gravity, and ecosystem entanglement"](https://savibm.com/blog/vendor-lock-in/). Your monitoring uses their observability tool. Your deployment goes through their hosting. Your team's IDE integrations assume their API. Untangling all of these at once creates a migration project that can stall an engineering team for quarters.

### Layer 2: Code Architecture Lock-In

AI-generated code from platform-specific tools often contains vendor-specific patterns, API calls, and architectural decisions that don't translate cleanly to other environments. When your codebase has hundreds of calls to a specific provider's API, switching means rewriting every integration point. The prompt formats, the response parsing, the error handling, the rate limit logic—[all of it is vendor-specific](https://savibm.com/blog/vendor-lock-in/).

One enterprise team estimated 4,200 engineering hours to migrate from one AI provider to another because their prompts, evaluation logic, and retry strategies all assumed a single vendor's API behavior. At an average developer cost of $75/hour, that's $315,000 in migration costs alone—not including the opportunity cost of features not built during the migration period.

### Layer 3: Knowledge and Workflow Lock-In

The most insidious form of lock-in is organizational. Your team learns the platform's quirks, builds workflows around its limitations, and develops institutional knowledge that only applies to that specific tool. As [AI consultant Haroon Choudery noted](https://www.theregister.com/software/2026/04/28/locked-stocked-and-losing-budget-ai-vendor-lock-in-bites/5229050), "Switching model vendors is no longer just an API migration. It is context, workflows, and institutional memory."

When you eventually need to migrate, you're not just moving code—you're retraining your entire team on a new development paradigm.

## When Platform Lock-In Makes Sense

To be fair, platform lock-in isn't always bad. For certain use cases, the convenience and integration of a fully managed platform outweigh the risks:

- **Short-Term Projects**: If you're building a landing page for a two-month marketing campaign, vendor lock-in is irrelevant. Use the fastest, cheapest tool available.
- **Non-Critical Applications**: Internal tools, proof-of-concepts, and experimental projects don't require the same level of vendor independence as customer-facing production systems.
- **Resource-Constrained Teams**: If you have no technical team and no budget for infrastructure, a managed platform might be your only viable option—at least initially.

The key is making an informed decision. Understand what you're trading away and have a migration plan before you need it.
