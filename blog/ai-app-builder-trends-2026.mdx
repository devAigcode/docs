## Who Is Building

The most striking shift in 2026 is who's building software. Across major AI app builder platforms, roughly 80% of users identify as being in non-technical roles. The fastest-growing cohorts aren't engineering teams — they're founders, marketers, operations people, designers, and small business owners.

Founders make up the single largest group of active builders on platforms like Lovable and Bolt.new. They're building MVPs, customer-facing products, and internal tooling — often solo or with tiny teams. One data scientist at Lovable built an internal analytics platform called Lovsight during a company hackathon. Within weeks, 90% of the 150-person company was using it. Product managers were running lift analyses, the sales team was sizing opportunities against usage data, and customer support had rich user context without filing engineering tickets. That pattern — domain experts building tools that serve their entire organization — is becoming the norm rather than the exception.

Marketers and growth teams are the second-fastest-growing cohort. They're building landing pages, A/B test dashboards, lead tracking systems, and content management workflows — tools that previously required engineering cycles and product manager buy-in. When a marketer can ship a working conversion tracking dashboard in an afternoon, the bottleneck shifts from "can engineering build this?" to "what should we build next?"

Small business owners represent the third major wave. Restaurants, retail shops, gyms, salons — people who never considered themselves software creators are now building booking systems, inventory trackers, loyalty programs, and customer portals. Most of these projects would never have justified a custom development budget. At $0 to $50/month and zero engineering overhead, the economics fundamentally change.

The geographic distribution is also shifting. While the United States, Western Europe, and India remain the largest markets by paid subscriptions, the fastest growth in 2026 is happening across South America and Africa. Colombia, Mexico, Brazil, and Nigeria are all seeing triple-digit growth rates on major platforms. Software creation is decoupling from traditional tech hubs — and the ideas emerging from these new centers of gravity look different from what Silicon Valley typically builds.

## What They Build

If you listened only to the hype cycle, you'd think AI app builders are mostly used for toy projects and weekend experiments. The data says otherwise. Across the major platforms, the most common project categories map directly to real business operations.

<Frame caption="What people build with AI app builders in 2026, ranked by share of projects">
  ![Horizontal bar chart of project categories built with AI app builders in 2026, led by websites and landing pages at 49 percent](/images/blog/ai-app-builder-trends-2026/01.JPEG "AI app builder project categories in 2026")
</Frame>

Websites and landing pages dominate — accounting for nearly half of all projects on platforms like Hostinger Horizons.[^citation](https://www.hostinger.com/blog/ai-app-builder-statistics) But these aren't simple brochure sites. Builders are shipping SEO-optimized pages with dynamic content, integrated payment flows, user authentication, and analytics. With the shift to server-side rendering on platforms like Lovable's TanStack Start stack, these sites are now fully indexable by search engines and show up properly in social previews — addressing what was historically a major limitation of AI-generated web apps.

The next tier is internal tools and operational systems. CRMs, inventory management systems, project trackers, and HR platforms are among the most common project types. These are the systems businesses run on — and increasingly, they're not being bought off the shelf. They're being built in-house by the people who will use them, tailored to workflows that generic SaaS never quite fits. When a 50-person company builds its own CRM in a weekend instead of paying $150 per seat per month for Salesforce, the ROI is immediate and obvious.

Booking and scheduling systems are particularly popular among service-based businesses. Salons, clinics, tutors, consultants — anyone who sells time — are building custom booking flows with calendar integration, automated reminders, and payment processing. Off-the-shelf scheduling tools exist, but they're rigid. A custom-built system that matches exactly how a specific business operates typically takes a day or two to build with current AI tooling.

Dashboards and data tools are the fourth major category. Companies are connecting their AI-built apps to databases, analytics platforms, and third-party APIs — BigQuery, PostHog, Stripe, HubSpot — and building unified views that surface exactly the metrics each team needs. This is effectively a new class of software: hyper-specific internal analytics tools that cost almost nothing to build and replace hours of manual data wrangling every week.

E-commerce and marketplace projects round out the top categories, accounting for roughly 10% of all projects.[^citation](https://www.hostinger.com/blog/ai-app-builder-statistics) Product catalogs, shopping carts, checkout flows, payment integration, order management — these are being built end-to-end by solo founders and small teams without dedicated engineering resources. The quality bar is rising quickly. A year ago, AI-generated e-commerce stores were clearly AI-generated. Today, the best ones are indistinguishable from professionally developed storefronts.

A smaller but rapidly growing category is agentic applications — apps that don't just present data but take action. Automated customer support systems, AI-powered content pipelines, and workflow automation tools that chain together multiple services. Twice as many teams built agentic products in 2025 compared to 2024, and the trend is accelerating.[^citation](https://www.hostinger.com/blog/ai-app-builder-statistics) This is where the tools are heading next: from building apps to building systems that build and operate themselves.

## Why They Build

Eight in ten builders on major platforms say they intend to monetize what they've built. Over half are actively building a business. Another quarter are working on side projects they hope to turn into revenue streams.

The motivations break down into four clear patterns.

Client demos and sales acceleration are the most common entry point. A founder pitches a prospective client, builds a working prototype over the weekend, and walks into the Monday meeting with something the client can actually click through. This collapses the traditional sales cycle — instead of selling a deck and a promise, you're selling a product that already exists. The conversion rate difference is dramatic, and the cost to produce that demo has dropped from tens of thousands of dollars to essentially zero.

Internal tools are the second major driver — and the category with the clearest, most defensible ROI. When a data scientist builds an analytics platform that eliminates 20 hours of manual reporting per week across a 150-person company, the math is straightforward. More commonly, it's a marketing team building their own campaign tracker instead of waiting six weeks for engineering bandwidth, or an operations manager shipping an inventory system that replaces a mess of Google Sheets. These projects don't show up on Product Hunt. They don't generate revenue directly. But they make the entire organization faster.

Side projects and portfolio builders represent about a quarter of active users. These are people experimenting with ideas, learning the tools, and building things that might become businesses. The interesting shift here is how many of these projects are reaching real users. Lovable projects collectively see an average of 720 million visits per month — meaning a substantial number of these "side projects" have actual audiences.

Full business launches are the end state for the most committed builders. Platforms are seeing users reach five- and six-figure revenue milestones on apps that started as weekend projects. Payment integrations went live on Lovable in February 2026, and within months, builders were generating meaningful income. The build economy is transitioning from potential to actual economic activity.

## What Blocks Them

For all the momentum, significant friction remains. The gap between "I built an app" and "I have a production-ready, secure, scalable product" is still real — and it's where most builders hit the wall.

<Frame caption="The five biggest blockers between an AI-built prototype and a production-ready product">
  ![Infographic of five AI app builder blockers: security, database, authentication, deployment, and SEO](/images/blog/ai-app-builder-trends-2026/02.JPEG "Top five blockers for AI-built apps in 2026")
</Frame>

Security is the single most frequently cited concern — and it's not unfounded. A Stanford randomized controlled trial found that developers using AI tools wrote demonstrably less secure code than those who didn't, while simultaneously reporting higher confidence in its security.[^citation](https://www.hostinger.com/blog/vibe-coding-statistics) A CodeRabbit analysis found AI-generated code contains 1.7 times more major issues than human-written code, including a 2.74 times higher rate of security vulnerabilities.[^citation](https://www.kristian-larsen.com/info/vibecoding-statistics/) The trust paradox — builders feeling more confident while producing less secure output — is arguably the defining challenge of vibe coding in 2026.

Database and backend complexity is the second major blocker. Building a frontend with natural language prompts is now trivially easy. Designing a database schema that won't collapse under real usage, implementing proper row-level security, managing migrations, and optimizing queries — these are fundamentally harder problems. Most AI app builders have gotten dramatically better at the frontend layer while still struggling with the backend. Builders frequently report hitting a wall when their app needs to move beyond basic CRUD operations.

Authentication and user management is the third pain point. Setting up email/password auth is straightforward with Supabase or similar backends. But the moment a builder needs role-based access control, SSO, team management, or fine-grained permissions, things get complicated fast. These features are table stakes for any serious business application, and they're still surprisingly difficult to implement correctly with current AI tooling.

Deployment and infrastructure represent an ongoing friction point. While platforms have made great strides — automated deployments, edge hosting, environment variable management — builders still encounter issues around custom domains, DNS configuration, scaling, monitoring, and production debugging. When something breaks at 2 AM, the non-technical builder who built the app in an afternoon often has no idea how to fix it.

SEO and discoverability rounds out the top concerns. Client-side rendered apps have historically been poorly indexed by search engines, and many AI-built projects still default to CSR architectures. Platforms are addressing this — Lovable's shift to TanStack Start with SSR and SSG, for example, has driven a 2.9% increase in organic search traffic and a 98.5% increase in AI crawler traffic across their domains. But for many builders, getting their app found is still harder than building it.

These blockers form a clear pattern: the tools have solved the creation problem but not the full lifecycle problem. Building version one of an app is astonishingly fast. Making it secure, scalable, and discoverable still requires expertise that most builders don't have.
