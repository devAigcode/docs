`NEW DROP` / `STREET REBELS` / `LIMITED DROP COLLECTION` / `NO RESTOCKS EVER` / `SHOP DROP`

No carousel of lifestyle shots. No "curated collection" copy. Just the drop energy, immediately. ## The Cart Is Where Trust Happens

Nobody thinks about the cart until it's broken. But in e-commerce, the payment journey begins the moment someone sees their total — not the moment they type in a credit card number.

The cart in this demo handles product selection, image and spec display, quantity changes, removals, total calculation, and discount codes — all before checkout. The right panel shows a `TRANSACTION SUMMARY`: base valuation, discount applied, logistics estimate, net total.

Here's the detail that actually matters: when someone types `WELCOME20`, the system shows `OVERRIDE ACTIVE` and applies 20% off. The UI makes the math visible before the customer commits. That's not a feature — it's the foundation of payment trust. People pay when they understand exactly what they're paying for.

## Don't Build Payment. Build the Payment Flow.

The biggest mistake with AI coding tools is asking for the entire payment system at once. "Add Stripe" is a terrible prompt. Instead, I broke it into pieces:

Cart total → discount validation → shipping selection → payment method selection → payment form → order creation → confirmation page → order history sync.

Each piece is small enough to inspect. Each failure is isolated. Each improvement is obvious.

The checkout then layers payment in two stages. First, the cart summary offers `CARD`, `STRIPE`, and `PAYPAL` as visible options before `INITIALIZE SECURE CHECKOUT`. Second, inside the checkout flow, the user picks shipping, then hits `PAYMENT AUTHORIZATION` with `STRIPE SECURE` and `PAYPAL GATEWAY`, card number, expiration, CVC, and name fields.

This mirrors how actual stores work: lock in the order → choose delivery → pick a payment channel. Three decisions, not one magic button.

## Shipping Changes Everything

Most demos treat payment as a standalone screen. In reality, the final amount depends on shipping.

The `LOGISTICS PROTOCOL` section offers Standard Delivery (5 days, $10) and Express (2 days, $25). Switch the option, the total updates. That means the payment system isn't just reading product prices — it's accounting for shipping, discounts, and the interaction between them.

AutoCoder placed logistics and payment authorization in the same flow by default. That's small but significant: it means the tool understood that shipping *is* part of the payment experience, not a separate thing before it.

## Confirmation Is Where You Prove It Worked

The payment journey doesn't end at "pay now." It ends when the customer feels certain the transaction went through.

The confirmation page hits three notes: `ORDER SECURED`, `TRANSACTION COMPLETE`, and a real order ID like `ORD-1782875875534-693`. Below that: itemized purchases, fulfillment details, shipping info.

For streetwear, this page pulls real weight. You've been telling customers "no restocks ever" and building scarcity into every drop. That creates buying anxiety. A clear confirmation — with an order ID they can screenshot — is the exhale after the drop.

And it doesn't stop there. The `MY ORDERS` page connects the purchase to the post-purchase experience: Total Archive, In Transit / Active, Secured / Delivered, with status filters (Pending, Confirmed, Shipped, Delivered, Cancelled) and action buttons per order. Your customer comes back a week later and can see exactly where their drop piece is.

Most AI-built demos end at "payment successful." This one lets the customer walk away and come back — which is what a real store has to do.

## The Real Lesson

If I had to give one piece of advice from this project: build the payment flow before you touch a real payment provider.

The workflow that actually works:

1. Cart and order summary
2. Discount codes and amount calculation
3. Shipping method selection
4. Payment method selection and form fields
5. Order confirmation page
6. Completed orders synced into My Orders
7. Then, and only then, wire up Stripe or PayPal

Everything before step 7 is user experience. Everything after is the infrastructure. Get the experience right first — the infrastructure decisions are clearer when you know exactly what flow they're supporting.

For production, the next layer obviously needs environment variables, webhook verification, idempotent order handling, proper error states, refund logic, and a security review. But [AutoCoder](https://www.autocoder.cc/platform?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=latest&utm_campaign=FashionEcommerce "Test your full e-commerce flow with AutoCoder.cc") gives you the entire flow to test against before you commit to any of that.
