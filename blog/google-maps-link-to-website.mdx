*How Chris used AutoCoder.cc to turn one McDonald's listing into a complete campaign site*

<Frame caption="The generated Caesar Sauce landing page, including campaign messaging and a restaurant-finder call to action.">
  ![McDonald's Caesar Sauce launch landing page generated by AutoCoder.cc with a Hail Caesar hero section and restaurant finder button](/images/blog/google-maps-link-to-website/01.png "Generated Caesar Sauce campaign landing page")

When McDonald's announced its new Caesar Sauce, Chris immediately wanted to see what a dedicated launch site could look like. Instead of starting with a blank page, collecting store details, writing copy, and building every section manually, he tested [AutoCoder.cc's](https://www.autocoder.cc/platform?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=latest&utm_campaign=MapsToWebsite "Build full-stack apps with AutoCoder.cc") new Google Maps workflow.

The idea was simple: choose a McDonald's location, paste its Google Maps link into AutoCoder.cc, describe the site he wanted, and let the product generate the first working version.

---

## Step 1 — Choose the business on Google Maps

Chris searched for McDonald's locations in New York and opened a listing. The Maps page already contained the basic business context: the store name, address, opening hours, phone number, photos, directions, ordering options, ratings, and customer reviews.

That information normally has to be gathered and reorganized before a business website can be built. Here, the listing itself became the source material. Chris copied the Google Maps URL and moved directly to AutoCoder.cc.

<Frame caption="Chris begins with a normal Google Maps search and selects a specific McDonald's location.">
  ![Google Maps search results for McDonald's locations in New York with a selected listing showing reviews and photos](/images/blog/google-maps-link-to-website/02.png "Google Maps McDonald's listing search")
</Frame>

---

## Step 2 — Paste the link and describe the result

Inside AutoCoder.cc, Chris pasted the Maps link and added a short instruction: build a launch website for McDonald's Caesar Sauce, with a bold hero section, a product lineup, flavor details, a restaurant finder, and frequently asked questions.

<Frame caption="Chris pastes the Google Maps link and campaign prompt into AutoCoder.cc.">
  ![AutoCoder.cc prompt box with a Google Maps link and instructions to build a McDonald's Caesar Sauce launch website](/images/blog/google-maps-link-to-website/03.PNG "Pasting a Google Maps link into AutoCoder.cc")
</Frame>

The link supplied the real-world business context. The prompt supplied the creative direction. AutoCoder.cc used both to decide what pages, components, calls to action, and content structure the project needed.

---

## Step 3 — Generate the product lineup

The generated site did more than display a restaurant name and address. It created a campaign-style product section with images, names, descriptions, pricing, key ingredients, nutritional details, and ordering actions. Each item was presented as a separate card, making the lineup easy to scan.

Because the structure was generated as part of the application, Chris could later change the content without rebuilding the page. The result felt like a dedicated product launch rather than a repackaged directory listing.

<Frame caption="AutoCoder.cc organizes the Caesar lineup into a structured, responsive product section.">
  ![Generated product lineup section showing four Caesar Sauce menu items as cards with pricing, key ingredients, and ordering actions](/images/blog/google-maps-link-to-website/04.PNG "Caesar Sauce product lineup section")
</Frame>

## Step 4 — Add location search and campaign information

Next, AutoCoder.cc generated the practical parts of the site. A ZIP-code search helped visitors find a nearby restaurant, while the FAQ section answered common questions about availability, ingredients, customization, and delivery.

This changed the page from a visual concept into a usable customer journey: discover the sauce, compare the products, understand the details, and then find a restaurant.

## Step 5 — Manage the campaign from an admin panel

AutoCoder.cc also created an admin panel behind the website. Chris could view campaign performance, see store-finder activity, review conversion metrics, and move between content-management areas for the hero section, products, FAQs, and campaign settings.

This was useful because the generated project was not limited to a static front end. It included the operational interface needed to update and manage the campaign after the initial build.

<Frame caption="The campaign dashboard provides a central view of traffic, store-finder clicks, and content controls.">
  ![Admin panel campaign dashboard with total launch visits, store finder clicks, conversion rate, and a traffic analytics chart](/images/blog/google-maps-link-to-website/06.png "Campaign analytics admin dashboard")

## Step 6 — Edit products without touching the layout

From the product-management screen, Chris could edit individual items, change their order, hide or remove products, and create new entries. A live preview at the bottom showed how the lineup would appear to visitors.

That separation matters: the design and application structure can stay consistent while campaign content changes. A new product, revised description, or different ordering sequence can be handled from the interface rather than through another full development cycle.
