## Before the Site, Everything Was Scattered

Running the restaurant was not hardest in the kitchen. It was hardest in the gaps between people.

Orders came from **messages**, **calls**, and **in-person changes**. Some were updated later. Nothing stayed in one clear flow.

During busy hours, the problem was not just volume. It was **coordination**.

- customers were waiting for updates
- riders were asking what was ready
- staff were checking notes, the kitchen, and each other

Inside the shop, small handoff problems kept piling up:

- an order detail could be missed
- a sold-out item could stay visible too long
- pickup update could depend on asking around
- the customer side and kitchen side could drift apart

None of this looked dramatic on its own. But together, it kept slowing the shop down.

  ![Small restaurant kitchen during rush hour with rider, customer, and staff coordinating by hand](/images/blog/multi-role-restaurant-platform/01.png)
</Frame>

One busy stretch made it impossible to ignore.

A rider was waiting at the front. A customer was asking for an update. Someone in the kitchen had just said one item was sold out. But the order had already moved forward on the customer side.

Nothing had fully failed. But all three sides were suddenly **out of sync at once**.

That was the moment the problem became clear. I did not just need a better-looking restaurant website. I needed one place that could hold the real flow of the shop.

A normal site could show the menu. It could not handle the work behind the order.

What I actually needed was one connected system for **customers**, **riders**, and **staff**.

That was the real starting point. I stopped thinking about a website as a front page. I started thinking about it as part of the operation itself.

## Start with What People Feel

### What the site should not become

Once I knew the site had to support **real work**, I set one limit early. I did not want a **generic ordering platform**.

This is a **small restaurant**. People come for more than food. They come for the **mood** too:

- **warm light**
- **wood tables**
- a **slower rhythm**

That feeling could not disappear **online**.

So I started with the **front**. Tone mattered first. A **cold** or overly **platform-like** site would feel wrong from the start.

## The Site Started to Work Once Each Role Stopped Guessing

The real shift was not the number of pages. It was that fewer parts of the day depended on **asking around**, **remembering details**, or **explaining the same thing twice**.

Before that, too much of the restaurant ran through interruption. Customers asked for updates. Riders asked what was ready. Staff kept filling the gaps by checking notes, checking the kitchen, and passing information forward by hand.

Once each role had its own place inside the same system, that changed. The work did not suddenly become easy, but it became easier to follow. More of the flow was visible. Less of it had to live in someone's head.

### For customers, the hardest part began after payment

Placing the order was only the first step. The harder part was everything after it.

Before this, customers could pay and still feel unsure about what was happening next. They had to guess:

- **Was the order confirmed?**
- **Was it being prepared?**
- **Was it almost ready?**

That uncertainty created a quiet kind of pressure inside the shop. During busy periods, staff kept answering the same update questions by hand, even when the order was already moving.

Once the customer side was in place, a lot of that checking shifted. People could log in, look at the order they already cared about, and see what was happening without asking the shop first.

<Frame caption="Customer dashboard — Welcome Back with an Active Kitchen order showing Pending, Preparing, Ready, Delivered stages">
  ![Customer dashboard showing Welcome Back to Sunny Pot Kitchen header, VIP member badge, and an Active Kitchen order card with order progress stages and a Track Order button](/images/blog/multi-role-restaurant-platform/07.png)
</Frame>

What made that page useful was not just visibility. It removed one routine interruption from the counter and moved it into the system.

Customers could now see:

- **order status**
- **timing**
- **the next step**

That did not erase every delay. It did something simpler and more useful: it made the wait easier to understand.

The tracking view pushed that one step further. Before, "Where is my order now?" had to be answered by someone in the restaurant. Afterward, much of that checking could happen on the customer side instead.

I also did not want the customer side to feel useful for only one order. Saved addresses solved a smaller problem, but a common one.

<Frame caption="Customer Saved Addresses — Home and Gym entries ready for faster return orders">
  ![Customer Saved Addresses page with Home and Gym address cards, each with Edit and Delete actions, plus an Add New Address button](/images/blog/multi-role-restaurant-platform/08.png)
</Frame>

That changed ordinary behavior in ordinary ways:

- **less repeated input**
- **faster return orders**
- **less friction on normal days**

So the customer side did more than make the flow look complete. It reduced small, repeated pieces of work on both sides.

### For riders, the problem was not action — it was uncertainty before action

Rider coordination used to begin with questions:

- **Is this one ready?**
- **Which order goes first?**
- **What is still waiting?**

The delay happened before delivery even started. Riders were ready to move, but the information they needed was often still trapped in conversation.

The rider changed that by making the task list visible as soon as they logged in.

<Frame caption="Rider dashboard — Available Deliveries and Active Tasks visible on login, no more asking around">
  ![Rider dashboard with Welcome back greeting, Shift Status toggle, Available Deliveries list with Accept Order buttons, and an Active Tasks panel showing a running pickup](/images/blog/multi-role-restaurant-platform/09.png)
</Frame>

Visibility helped, but the bigger improvement was structure. The page separated:

- **what could be accepted**
- **what was already in progress**

That small distinction changed the rider's first step. Instead of stopping to confirm the situation again, they could read it and act on it.

The benefit was not only on the rider side. It also took pressure off the counter. Riders no longer had to wait for someone to explain what was ready, what was moving, and what still needed time. The system was carrying more of that handoff on its own.

### For staff, the biggest improvement was fewer fragile handoffs

This was the part that mattered most to me.

Running the shop was never about one isolated task. It was about too many transitions happening at once:

- **new order**
- **kitchen progress**
- **sold-out item**
- **rider assignment**
- **completion**

That is where the day usually started to break apart. Not in one dramatic failure, but in the small gaps between steps.

The order queue was the first place where the system began to feel real.

<Frame caption="Staff Orders Queue — one shared view across New Orders, In Kitchen, and Dispatch columns">
  ![Staff dashboard showing Hello Sunny Staff header and an Orders Queue view split into New Orders, In Kitchen, and Dispatch columns with order cards and action buttons](/images/blog/multi-role-restaurant-platform/010.png)

It gave the team one shared view of the flow:

- **what had just come in**
- **what was being prepared**
- **what was moving toward dispatch**

The rush did not disappear. But staff no longer had to rebuild the state of the day from scattered pieces every time service got busy.

Menu management solved another recurring gap. In a real restaurant, items sell out. Small edits happen. Availability changes during service. Before, those changes could stay inside the shop while the customer-facing side still showed the older version.

<Frame caption="Staff Menu Management — inline item status, edit, and Sold Out controls keep the menu and kitchen in sync">

Once item status could be updated in one place, the menu and the kitchen stayed closer in sync. That reduced correction work and made it less likely that someone would order something the shop no longer had.

The same thing happened with rider assignment. Dispatch used to sit in an awkward middle space between kitchen progress and delivery movement. When that step became visible inside the same flow, it stopped depending so heavily on verbal coordination.

<Frame caption="Staff Rider Assignment — orders needing delivery matched against active riders in one view">

Later, the daily summary started to matter too. At first, I only cared about getting through service. After a while, I started to care about understanding what the day had actually looked like once the rush was over.

<Frame caption="Staff Daily Summary — revenue, order volume, delivery mix, rider activity, and top-selling items in one place">

That page helped with that. It showed:

- **order volume**
- **delivery mix**
- **rider activity**
- **popular items**

It did not make the kitchen faster in the moment. What it did was leave behind a clearer record of the operation, instead of letting the whole day blur together.

### What actually changed

I never needed a longer feature list. I needed fewer moments where one role had to stop and ask another role what was going on.

- Customers needed to see **where the order stood**
- Riders needed to see **what they could act on**
- Staff needed **one connected flow instead of scattered updates**

Once that happened, the system started taking over part of the coordination work that people had been carrying by hand.

That was the point when the site stopped feeling like a concept. It started to feel like **part of the restaurant's real operation**.
