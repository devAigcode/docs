## The Bottleneck Before the World Cup

For Daniel, the World Cup was never just a football event. It was the rare moment when fans, communities, brands, media partners, teams, and event operators all paid attention to the same thing at the same time. For a small football business owner, that attention was valuable — but difficult to capture.

Daniel had been building his business slowly. He ran a football community, published match previews and player stories, hosted fan discussions, and sometimes worked with a merchandise partner. The business was real, but scattered.

His audience was spread across social platforms, group chats, and manual updates. Match discussions disappeared quickly. Product links were shared one by one. Fixture posts became outdated. When fans asked about schedules, teams, tickets, or gear, Daniel had to reply with separate links and screenshots.

But the bigger bottleneck was not only on the fan side.

When Daniel talked to fans, he needed to show schedules, scores, stories, community discussions, tickets, and merchandise. When he talked to team or event partners, he needed to show how the platform could support preparation, fixtures, logistics, and operational coordination. When he talked to media partners, he needed a place for news, interviews, analysis, and content distribution. When he talked to brands and sponsors, he needed to show audience value, traffic entry points, user behavior, and commercial conversion. When he looked at his own business, he needed a way to manage events, ticketing, merchandise, content, community, and platform data.

As the World Cup approached, the gap became obvious: the opportunity was growing, but his system was not.

Daniel did not only need a fan website. He needed a prototype that could make a bigger business system visible. That was why he started building FIFA Hub with [AutoCoder](https://www.autocoder.cc/platform?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=latest&utm_campaign=WorldCupPlatform "Build a clickable prototype with AutoCoder").

Instead of preparing another presentation, he turned his World Cup idea into a clickable multi-role platform prototype. The first important screen was not just a homepage. It was a role-based entry point.

## From an Idea to a Multi-Role World Cup Platform Prototype

Daniel did not start with a complete product plan. He only had a rough picture: a place where fans could follow the tournament, read stories, check fixtures, discover teams, join discussions, and eventually buy tickets or merchandise.

But as he shaped the idea, the platform became larger than a fan destination.

The World Cup is not experienced by one type of user. Fans want emotion, schedules, stories, and products. Team staff need coordination, match preparation, squad information, and logistics. Players need clarity around schedules, appearances, media duties, and important alerts. Media partners need content structure and publishing space. Administrators need control over events, users, revenue, content, and system status.

A written plan could not show that. A spreadsheet could not show the user journey. A slide deck could explain the opportunity, but it could not make different stakeholders experience their own role inside the platform.

With AutoCoder, Daniel could describe the platform in natural language, define user roles, outline core pages, and quickly turn the idea into a clickable product experience.

The result was FIFA Hub: a multi-role World Cup platform prototype that connected the key parts of the tournament experience.

The homepage created the first layer of the platform. It gave fans a strong World Cup entry point, with tournament atmosphere, featured matches, stories, teams, fixtures, and platform navigation. It helped Daniel show that FIFA Hub could become a central destination for World Cup attention.

## Designing the Growth Layer: Fans, Content, Tickets, and Commerce

After the prototype had a clear structure, Daniel began thinking less like a content creator and more like a product owner.

He asked one question: when a fan enters FIFA Hub during the World Cup, what should happen next?

The answer was not just "show information." Fans already had many places to find scores and news. FIFA Hub needed to guide users through a complete growth journey: attention, utility, content engagement, commercial conversion, and return visits.

This became the first layer of the platform: the fan-facing growth layer.

<Frame caption="A signed-in fan landing on the digital arena — the start of a guided journey from attention to engagement.">

The homepage captured attention, but fans needed reasons to come back. That made the fixtures page important.

On the match fixtures page, fans could check upcoming games, filter by team, group, stage, venue, or match status, and connect each match with stadium and ticket information. A simple schedule became a decision tool. A fan could see the next match, understand the venue, check ticketing status, and decide what to do next in one place.

During the World Cup, fans do not only watch games. They read previews, follow interviews, look for tactical analysis, and search for stories around their favorite teams. The News and Media page gave Daniel a place to organize that content properly.

Instead of posting everything separately across social platforms, he could build a more complete editorial experience. Match previews, interviews, tactical breakdowns, and feature stories all had a clear place. This also made the platform more valuable to media partners, because content was no longer scattered across temporary posts. It had a structured publishing environment.

## Building Role-Based Workflows: Fan, Team Staff, Player, Media, and Admin

Daniel did not only need a beautiful homepage. He needed to show different people why the platform mattered to them.

Fans needed excitement and utility. Team staff needed coordination. Players needed clarity. Media partners needed content structure. Admins needed control. With [AutoCoder](https://www.autocoder.cc/platform?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=latest&utm_campaign=WorldCupPlatform "Prototype multi-role workflows with AutoCoder"), Daniel could prototype all of these roles before building the full system.

This became the second layer of FIFA Hub: the role-specific workflow layer.

One example was the Team Staff view.

This page focused on practical tournament information: the next fixture, stadium details, logistics notes, squad readiness, and current stage standing. For fans, a match is something to watch. For team staff, the same match is a planning task. They need to know where the team is going, what must be prepared, and how the current standing affects the next decision.

The media role added another angle. Media partners did not only need to read news. They needed a place to manage stories, publish interviews, organize match analysis, and distribute content around teams and fixtures. ## The Business Outcome: A Prototype That Drives Growth and Revenue

After Daniel launched FIFA Hub, the biggest change was that his audience finally had one clear place to go.

Before the prototype, fans were scattered across social posts, group chats, external store links, and temporary match updates. After the prototype, they could enter one platform to check fixtures, follow tournament data, read stories, join discussions, and discover merchandise.

Within the first three weeks of the campaign, FIFA Hub reached 18,400 unique visitors and generated 72,000 page views. The match fixtures page and tournament data page became the strongest traffic drivers, accounting for 46% of total visits.

This confirmed one of Daniel's most important assumptions: fans did not only want content. They wanted useful World Cup tools they could return to.

The platform also created stronger retention than his previous social media campaigns. Average session time reached 4 minutes and 18 seconds, and 38% of users returned within seven days. For a small football business, this was a clear sign that the platform was not just attracting attention — it was building repeat behavior.

Community activity gave Daniel another signal. During the campaign, 1,260 fans created accounts, 430 discussion threads were posted, and the most active match thread received more than 1,800 views. Conversations that used to disappear in group chats were now becoming part of his own audience asset.

The commercial results were also concrete.

The merchandise store generated 186 orders and $6,740 in sales, mainly from jerseys, match balls, and fan accessories. Ticket-related pages drove 740 outbound ticket clicks and helped Daniel earn $8,920 in ticketing partnership revenue.

More importantly, the prototype helped him close two small sponsorship packages worth $12,000 in total. Sponsors could finally see where their campaigns would appear, how fans would move through the platform, and how match attention could become measurable engagement.

For Daniel, FIFA Hub was no longer just a website concept.

It became a working business prototype: one that brought fans in, kept them engaged, created community activity, opened revenue channels, and gave partners something concrete to understand.
